### Historical Explanations

This diary entry, dated late 1923, captures a moment of extreme crisis in Germany's Weimar Republic.

*   **Hyperinflation (1923):** The entry is a vivid first-hand account of hyperinflation. The author notes astronomical exchange rates ("1 Pfund Sterling kostet nun 30 Billionen Mark", "1 frz. Franc erfordert ungefähr 1 Billion = 1.000.000.000.000- Mark"). This rampant inflation was caused by the government printing massive amounts of money to pay war reparations mandated by the Treaty of Versailles and to finance passive resistance against the occupation of the Ruhr. As the author describes, the currency became worthless, savings were wiped out, and the economy reverted to a barter system ("anders verkauft der Landwirt nicht mehr"). The date of the last entry, November 25, 1923, is significant. The hyperinflation was officially ended just days earlier with the introduction of a new, stable currency, the **Rentenmark** (on November 15-20). The author's feeling of facing an "ungewissen, wahrscheinlich aber schrecklichen Zukunft" perfectly reflects the national mood at this turning point, as it was not yet clear if the new currency would succeed.

*   **Occupation of the Ruhr & Rhineland Separatism:** The entry mentions that a territory is "von Italien besetzt" (occupied by Italy). This refers to the **Occupation of the Ruhr** (January 1923 – 1925). French and Belgian troops occupied Germany's industrial heartland to seize goods as payment for reparations. Italy, an Allied power, sent a small technical delegation, hence their mention. This occupation triggered passive resistance, economic collapse, and political turmoil. As the author notes, this instability fueled separatist movements. In the occupied Rhineland, some groups, with encouragement from the French, attempted to declare an independent **"Rheinische Republik"** separate from Germany. These attempts, as the diary correctly states, ultimately failed due to a lack of popular support and the eventual stabilization of the German state.

*   **The League of Nations (Völkerbund):** The author's skepticism towards the League of Nations ("sogen. 'Völkerbund'") was widespread in Germany. The organization was seen as a tool of the victorious Allied powers, unable or unwilling to act fairly in disputes involving Germany.
